Backstories

The "why" behind the writing system. None of this is needed to order a coffee, but it makes the strange parts stop feeling arbitrary, which is its own kind of memory aid. Lessons link here for the fuller story; this page grows as we go.

Why three scripts?

Japan had no writing of its own. Kanji (Chinese characters) arrived from China around the 5th century and were the first script.1 But Chinese and Japanese are built differently: Chinese has no way to write Japanese grammar (the little endings and particles like は, を). So scribes started using certain kanji purely for their sound, ignoring meaning, to spell out Japanese. That workaround was called man'yōgana (8th century).

Writing whole kanji just for sound was slow, so man'yōgana got simplified two different ways, by two different groups, at roughly the same time (9th–10th century):

  • Hiragana — the flowing, rounded script. It came from writing man'yōgana in fast cursive. It was associated with women's and literary writing at court (the Tale of Genji was written in it).2 Today it writes native words and all the grammar.
  • Katakana — the sharp, angular script. Buddhist monks made it as shorthand, snipping off a fragment of a man'yōgana character as an annotation aid.3 Today it writes loanwords.

So the modern division of labour is: kanji = meaning-roots, hiragana = native words + grammar glue, katakana = foreign borrowings. Three scripts, three jobs.

Thai parallel. Thai also took its script from an Indian (Brahmic) source and adapted it to a language it didn't originally fit. Borrowing a neighbour's writing system and bending it is a very normal thing for a language to do.

Why is a word borrowed vs invented?

Japanese borrowed in two big waves, and that's why some foreign ideas are written in kanji and others in katakana:

  • Old Chinese loans (called kango) are ancient and written in kanji — they've been part of the language for over a thousand years and don't feel foreign.
  • Western loans (called gairaigo) came later and are written in katakana. Portuguese traders arrived in the 1500s, the Dutch during Japan's closed-border Edo period, then English became the top source from the 1800s on. Today about 80% of gairaigo are from English.4
  • Sometimes Japan invented a kanji compound instead of borrowing the sound. In the Meiji modernisation (late 1800s), scholars coined new words from kanji for Western concepts: 電話 denwa = "electric + talk" = telephone; 経済 keizai = economy. These look native because they're built from Chinese roots, even though the concept was imported.
📜 Backstory · your two languages, one Portuguese word

パン (pan) = "bread" came into Japanese from Portuguese pão in the 1500s. Thai ปัง is usually traced to the same Portuguese word, from the same century of Portuguese traders (they were in Ayutthaya too). Two languages, one borrowing. It's written in katakana precisely because it's a foreigner.

Why does this word have a kanji version?

Kanji carry meaning, so most content words got a kanji assigned whose meaning matched. 水 means "water," and the native word mizu is written with it. But:

  • Loanwords have no kanji — コーヒー is a foreign sound with no Japanese meaning-root, so it stays in katakana. That's the quickest tell that a word was borrowed.
  • Grammar words usually drop their kanji. ください technically has a kanji form (下さい) and すみません has one too (済みません), but they're so common and so grammatical that they're normally written in kana. When in doubt, function words lean kana.
  • The polite お prefix. お水 (o-mizu), お会計 (o-kaikei): that お is a politeness marker glued to the front of a noun. You'll hear it constantly in shops and restaurants, where staff speak extra-politely.

Why the little dots (dakuten ゛)?

The two-dot mark that turns カ (ka) into ガ (ga) is a diacritic called dakuten. It marks a voiced consonant (k→g, s→z, t→d, h→b). For much of Japanese writing history it was optional: older texts frequently left it off and trusted the reader to tell カ from ガ by context. Consistent, required use of dakuten only settled in relatively modern times.5 So "same shape plus two dots" isn't a shortcut — it's a diacritic that got layered on later, which is why the base chart and its voiced partners share their shapes.
