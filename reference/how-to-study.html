How to make it stick

Your instinct for flashcards is right. Here is why it works, and the three research-backed moves that make cheap practice turn into real memory. Your style + these methods = the whole study system.

The one distinction that matters

There are two kinds of "knowing":

  • Fluency — you recognise it right now because you just read it. Feels like mastery. Fades fast.
  • Storage — you can pull it up cold, days later. This is the real goal, and it only comes from a bit of struggle.

Re-reading a lesson builds fluency and fools you. The drills below build storage.

Three moves (built into every lesson)

1. Retrieval — recall, don't review

Always try to produce the answer from memory before you check. The little effort of retrieval is what writes it to long-term memory. That's why the flashcard drill makes you flip to check rather than showing both sides at once, and why there's a type-in recall box. Getting it wrong and correcting is more effective than getting it right easily.

2. Spacing — spread it out

Ten minutes today, tomorrow, and in three days beats an hour once. Each time you let a memory almost fade and then retrieve it, it comes back stronger. So: do a lesson's drill now, then hit ↻ Restart on it tomorrow and again later in the week. Short and often wins, which suits your minimum-free-time constraint perfectly.

3. Interleaving — mix it up

Don't drill one lesson to death in isolation. Once you have two or three lessons, shuffle across them. Mixing patterns and words (rather than blocking one at a time) is harder in the moment and that difficulty is exactly what makes it durable. The drill randomises order for this reason; later we'll build mixed-review decks across lessons.

Your tailored moves (layered on top)

  • Flashcard drill — repeatable and randomised, both directions (read 日本語 → meaning, or English → say it). This is your main daily loop. See it live in any lesson.
  • Thai anchors — map each new sound to a Thai syllable you already own, so only the shape is new. See Your Thai head start.
  • Manga + games as spaced retrieval — you already look at Japanese daily. Sound out manga furigana; decode game-menu katakana. That's free, real-world practice between lessons.
