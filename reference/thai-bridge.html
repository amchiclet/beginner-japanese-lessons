Tourist Japanese · Reference

Your Thai head start

You already speak a tonal, particle-using, classifier-using, subject-dropping, loanword-adapting language with an unfamiliar script. That is most of the hard mental furniture Japanese needs. Here is what transfers, and the few things to watch.

Honesty note. I looked for kana that look like Thai letters and won't invent matches that aren't really there. The real leverage is sound and structure, below. That is where your advantage is large.

1. Pronunciation is almost free for you

Japanese has just 5 vowels, and every one is a sound you already say in Thai. Map each kana to a Thai syllable you already pronounce perfectly, and the shape is all that's new.

Two advantages English speakers don't get:
  • Long vs short vowels change meaning in Japanese (obasan = aunt, obāsan = grandma). Thai has phonemic vowel length (อะ vs อา) so your ear already hears this. The macron (ō, ū) just means "hold it longer."
  • The Japanese "R" (ら り る れ ろ) is a light flap, like Thai ร tapped once — not the English R everyone else struggles with.

2. Particles are just like Thai's little words

Thai ends sentences with politeness particles (ครับ / ค่ะ) and uses small words to mark role. Japanese does the same. So "a small word after the noun marks its job" is your native intuition, not a foreign rule.

3. Counters = Thai classifiers

In Thai you say "coffee two cups" (กาแฟสองแก้ว) — the classifier แก้ว. Japanese does the exact same thing with counters (二つ futatsu, 一枚 ichi-mai). English speakers find this baffling; you already think this way. We'll add the handful of tourist counters later.

4. Loanwords work like Thai loanwords

Thai bends English into Thai sounds: กาแฟ (coffee), เบียร์ (beer), แท็กซี่ (taxi). Katakana does the identical job: コーヒー (kōhī), ビール (bīru), タクシー (takushī). Once you read katakana, hundreds of menu and sign words are just English wearing a costume — a costume you already know how to read through.

5. Manga + games are free reading practice

You already look at Japanese daily. Use it as spaced retrieval:

  • Manga furigana — the small kana printed above kanji. That is a built-in reading trainer: sound out the kana you're learning, right there in the panel.
  • Game menus are pure katakana — スタート (start), セーブ (save), ロード (load), アイテム (item), メニュー (menu), レベル (level). You've read these shapes a thousand times without decoding them. Now you will.
  • Words you likely already know passively from anime/games: ありがとう (thank you), すごい (amazing), かわいい (cute), だめ (no good), やめて (stop). We'll formalise these — you're not starting from zero.

Watch out for (where Thai misleads)

  • Word order. Thai is verb-in-the-middle (ฉันกินข้าว, "I eat rice"). Japanese puts the verb last (私はご飯を食べる, "I rice eat"). For our fixed tourist patterns this is handled for you, but notice it: the sentence resolves at the end.
  • Tones. Thai has 5 lexical tones. Japanese does not — it has a lighter pitch-accent. Don't sing Thai tones onto Japanese words. A flat, even delivery is safer, and tone/pitch slips almost never stop a tourist from being understood.
  • Doubled (small っ) consonants and held vowels are their own beat. きて (kite, "come") vs きって (kitte, "stamp") — the pause is a full beat, like a deliberate Thai short-syllable stop.
