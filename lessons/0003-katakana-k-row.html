カ キ ク ケ コ — reading whole words

One consonant row on top of your five vowels, and suddenly you can read entire katakana words: ケーキ (cake), ココア (cocoa), キウイ (kiwi), オーケー (OK). This is the row where reading starts to feel real.

Warm-up: recall from before

Don't peek at the old lessons. Pull these from memory first, then tap to check. Mixing old and new like this is harder in the moment, and that difficulty is exactly what makes it stick (why).

The K-row

Each of these is just k + a vowel you already know. The vowel sound is unchanged from Lesson 2, so you're really only learning five new shapes. Tap 🔊, say each aloud.

KatakanaSound🇹🇭 Thai anchorShape hookSay
カkaกา (ก, not ค/ข)a slash through a corner
キkiกีlooks like a ki-y (key)
クkuกุan open beak going "coo"
ケkeเกhalf a capital K
コkoโกa square bracket / open box
Thai sound note. Japanese k is unaspirated, so it's closer to Thai ก (as in ก ไก่) than to the puffed ค/ข. Your ก is a near-perfect match. And you've already met コ: it's the first character of コーヒー from Lesson 1.

Now read whole words

Vowels + long dash + K-row is enough to fully decode real words. Read each left to right, one mora at a time, before you tap 🔊.

WordSound it outMeansSay
ケーキke + ー + ki = kēkicake
ココアko + ko + a = kokoacocoa
キウイki + u + i = kiuikiwi
オーケーo + ー + ke + ー = ōkēOK
You just read four words with zero memorisation of meaning. You decoded the sounds and the English word fell out. That's the whole katakana game.

Bonus: two dots that double your reach (ダクテン)

Add a little ゛ mark (called dakuten, "two dots") to a K-row character and it becomes its voiced partner: the hard "k" softens to a "g". Same shapes you just learned, one new rule.

Plain+ ゛ =SoundSay
カ ka→ガga
キ ki→ギgi
ク ku→グgu
ケ ke→ゲge
コ ko→ゴgo

Now the ゲ in ゲーム (game, from Lesson 2) makes sense: it's just ケ + ゛. And you can read ギア (gia) = "gear" .

📜 Backstory · why ガ is just カ with quotation marks

Those two dots are a diacritic bolted onto the base kana to mark a voiced sound. For much of their history they were optional: older texts often left them out and trusted the reader to know from context whether a word was カ or ガ. Consistent use of dakuten only settled in fairly modern times. So the "same shape, plus dots" pattern isn't sloppiness, it's a diacritic layered on later. Fuller story →
