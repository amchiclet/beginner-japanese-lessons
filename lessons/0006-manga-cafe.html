ネットカフェ — manga cafes & tight spaces

Manga cafes (ネットカフェ / 漫画喫茶) are a great cheap place to rest, read, or even sleep, but the front desk is often all-Japanese and the flow is not like a hotel. This lesson gives you the specific phrases for registering, picking a room type, and handling the small shared bathroom.

Warm-up: recall from before

From memory first, then tap to check.

It isn't "check-in" — it's registration

You flagged this exactly right. A manga cafe doesn't run hotel-style "check-in." For a first visit you register as a member (free, one short form), then pick a plan and a seat. The word for the front desk is 受付 (uketsuke), a good sign to recognise.

JapaneseRomajiMeans / whenSay
受付uketsukereception / front desk (a sign)
初めてですhajimete desu"It's my first time" — signals you need to register
会員登録をお願いしますkaiin tōroku o onegaishimasu"I'd like to register as a member"
チェックアウトをお願いしますchekku-auto o onegaishimasu"I'd like to check out" (leaving — this one is a loanword)
New polite word: お願いします (onegaishimasu) . It's a softer, broader "please" than ください. Use ください to ask for a thing (コーヒーをください); use お願いします to request an action or service (会員登録をお願いします). When unsure, お願いします is almost always safe.

Coming back as a member (and how hotels differ)

Once you're registered, a return visit is not a hotel-style "check-in." You show your member card at the 受付 and pick a plan. There's no special check-in word to learn here.

JapaneseRomajiWhenSay
会員ですkaiin desu"I'm a member" (show the card)
会員カードkaiin kādo"member card" (hand it over)
予約していますyoyaku shite imasu"I have a reservation" (if you booked)
Does Japanese use the word "check in"? Yes — but only for hotels. チェックイン (chekku-in) is a normal katakana loanword, used exactly like English, at hotels and ryokan. A manga cafe front desk won't use it. So:
  • Hotel: 予約しています。チェックインをお願いします。 (Yoyaku shite imasu. Chekku-in o onegaishimasu.) = "I have a reservation. Check-in, please."
  • Manga cafe: just 会員です + show the card. No "check-in."
A full hotel lesson can come later; this is the one phrase to carry for now.

What the clerk might ask you (listening)

Staff speak in keigo (super-polite service Japanese), so their questions sound longer and softer than what you say back. Don't try to parse the whole sentence. Catch the one keyword and give a short answer you already know. Tap 🔊 to train your ear.

They say (🔊)Listen forThey're askingYou answer
会員でいらっしゃいますか？
kaiin de irasshaimasu ka?		 会員"Are you a member?" 会員です / 初めてです
会員カードをお願いします。
kaiin kādo o onegaishimasu		 カード"Your member card, please." hand over the card (はい)
初めてのご利用ですか？
hajimete no go-riyō desu ka?		 初めて"Is it your first time?" はい、初めてです
ご予約はされていますか？
go-yoyaku wa sarete imasu ka?		 予約"Do you have a reservation?" 予約しています / いいえ
お一人様ですか？
o-hitori-sama desu ka?		 一人"Just one person?" はい (nod)
喫煙ですか、禁煙ですか？
kitsuen desu ka, kin'en desu ka?		 禁煙"Smoking or non-smoking?" 禁煙 (non-smoking)
The whole trick. Their sentence is long and polite; your keyword is short. 会員? → 会員です. カード? → hand it over. 初めて? → 初めてです. 予約? → 予約しています. You'll understand far more than you can say, and that's exactly right for a tourist.
📜 Backstory · a "manga tea-house"

漫画喫茶 (manga kissa) = 漫画 (manga) + 喫茶 (kissa, "tea-drinking"). 喫茶店 (kissaten) is the old word for a traditional coffee-and-tea shop, and manga cafes borrowed the term. So the name literally reads "manga tea-house," even though today it's more about reclining chairs and free drink bars than tea. More backstories →

Choosing a seat (room types)

You'll be asked which プラン (puran, plan/package) and seat you want. Point at a photo if the words don't land, but these are the terms:1

JapaneseRomajiWhat it isSay
オープン席ōpun sekiopen seat — a desk chair, no walls (cheapest)
リクライニング席rikurainingu sekireclining seat — a chair that leans back
フラット席furatto sekiflat seat — flat floor to lie down on
個室koshitsuprivate room — walls + a door that locks
ナイトパックnaito pakkunight pack — cheap overnight block (often 8+ hrs)

Notice you can read a lot of these already: 席 (seki) = "seat," and the rest are katakana loanwords (オープン, リクライニング, フラット, ナイト, パック). To ask if there's a shower, reuse Pattern C from Lesson 5:

シャワーはありますか？
Shawā wa arimasu ka?  ·  "Do you have a shower?"

The small shared bathroom

Bathrooms here (and in many tight Japanese spaces) are small, and you'll share them. Two questions and one sign handle almost everything. Watch the stall indicator, and if you're unsure whether someone is waiting, just ask.

JapaneseRomajiMeansSay
空いていますか？aite imasu ka?"Is it free / vacant?"
並んでいますか？narande imasu ka?"Are you in line?" (ask the person standing there)
使用中shiyōchū"in use / occupied" — the stall-lock sign
お先にどうぞosaki ni dōzo"after you / go ahead" — yielding politely
The one sign to know: a stall showing 使用中 (shiyōchū) is occupied; if it shows 空 (aki) it's vacant. And in a tight doorway, すみません (excuse me, from Lesson 1) plus a small nod does most of the social work.
