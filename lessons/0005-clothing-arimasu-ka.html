___ はありますか？ — shopping for clothes

Your third and final core pattern: asking whether a shop has something. It's the exact tool you need in a clothing store, where Japanese sizing runs small and you'll often need a bigger size, another colour, or a fitting room. Now you'll have all three core patterns in hand.

Warm-up: recall from before

From memory first, then tap to check.

The pattern (Pattern C)

___ は あります か？
___ wa arimasu ka?  ·  "Do you have ___? / Is there ___?"
literally: [thing] + は (topic) + あります (exists) + か (question)

Same shape as Lesson 4's "where is ___?", just swap どこです for あります. You already own は and か. The one new word is あります (arimasu), "exists / there is." This completes the set:

PatternUseFrom
___ をくださいrequest / order itLesson 1
___ はどこですか？locate itLesson 4
___ はありますか？ask if they have itthis lesson
It works everywhere, not just clothes. Wi-Fiはありますか？ (do you have Wi-Fi?) · 英語のメニューはありますか？ (do you have an English menu?). One pattern, endless uses.

In the clothing store

When you walk in, staff will call out いらっしゃいませ (irasshaimase) = "welcome!" You don't reply to it. If you're only browsing, you can say 見ているだけです (mite iru dake desu) = "just looking."

The three sentences that cover most of it

これ、試着できますか？
Kore, shichaku dekimasu ka?  ·  "Can I try this on?"
もっと大きいサイズはありますか？
Motto ōkii saizu wa arimasu ka?  ·  "Do you have a bigger size?" (motto = more)
別の色はありますか？
Betsu no iro wa arimasu ka?  ·  "Do you have another colour?"

For a smaller size, swap in 小さい: これより小さいサイズはありますか？ = "Do you have a size smaller than this?"

Two things that surprise tourists. (1) Japanese sizes run small — a Japanese L is often a Western M, so expect to ask for 大きい (bigger). (2) In the fitting room you usually take your shoes off before stepping in, and there's often a face cover to pull over your head so makeup doesn't rub onto the clothes.1
📜 Backstory · 試着 = "try + wear"

試着 (shichaku) is built from 試 ("try, test") + 着 ("wear, put on") — literally "try-wear." The 着 here is the same "wear" idea you'll see all over clothing and laundry signs. Kanji compounds like this are little definitions: read the parts, get the meaning. More on kanji logic →
