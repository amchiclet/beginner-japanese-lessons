Tourist Japanese · Lesson 1

これをください — "This, please."

By the end of this page you can walk into any shop, café, or restaurant, point at what you want, and complete the whole exchange politely. One pattern, one power word, five nouns. That is a real transaction on day one.

The one word that does everything: すみません

すみません  (sumimasen)

This is your Swiss-army word. It means "excuse me" and you use it to get a waiter's attention, squeeze past someone, or open almost any interaction. Say this first, every time, and you already sound polite.1

Sound it out slowly: su-mi-ma-sen. Every letter is pronounced, vowels are pure: a="ah", i="ee", u="oo", e="eh".

Thai anchor. Every sound here is one you already say: su=สุ, mi=มิ, ma=มะ, sen=เซ็น. Only the kana shapes are new, not the sounds. More on this in Your Thai head start.

The pattern: ___ を ください

___ を ください
___ o kudasai  ·  "___, please" / "I'll have ___"
literally: [the thing] + を (marks it as what you want) + ください (please give me)

Two pieces do the work:

  • ください (kudasai) = "please give me." It ends the request and makes it polite.
  • を (o) = the object particle. It clips onto the end of the thing you want and says "this is the item." This is the closest thing Japanese has to your "articles": instead of a/the before a word, a particle comes after it to mark its job.2
Spelling quirk to file away, not memorise yet: the particle を is written with its own character but pronounced just like o. You'll meet it properly on the reading track.

Point and win: これ

The single most useful noun for a tourist is "this." Point at the dish in the photo menu, the pastry in the case, the ticket on the screen:

これをください。

Kore o kudasai. — "This, please."

Put it together with your power word and you have a complete, polite exchange:

すみません、これをください。
Sumimasen, kore o kudasai.  ·  "Excuse me, this please."

Five nouns to drop in the slot

KanaRomajiMeaningSay
これkorethis (point at it)
コーヒーkōhīcoffee
お水o-mizuwater
ビールbīrubeer
お会計o-kaikeithe bill / check

So コーヒーをください (kōhī o kudasai) = "coffee, please." And when you're done eating, お会計をください (o-kaikei o kudasai) = "the bill, please."

📜 Backstory · the お on お水 and お会計

That little お in front of お水 and お会計 is a politeness prefix glued to the noun. Plain 水 (mizu) is just "water"; お水 is the polite "water" you'll hear from shop and restaurant staff, who speak extra-politely to customers. Also notice: 水 has a kanji (it's a native word with a meaning-root), but コーヒー has none, because it's a borrowed foreign sound. That kana-vs-kanji split is your quickest clue to a word's origin. More on why →

Notice the loanwords. コーヒー and ビール are English-ish words written in katakana, the pointy script. Japan is full of these on menus and signs. That is exactly why we start the reading track with katakana next lesson.
