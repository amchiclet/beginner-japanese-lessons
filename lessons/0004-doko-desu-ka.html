Tourist Japanese · Lesson 4 · Speaking track

___ はどこですか？ — "Where is ___?"

Your second core pattern, and maybe the most reassuring one to have on a trip. With it you can find the toilet, the station, the exit, your hotel: anything, anywhere, from anyone. Plus your first survival kanji for reading the signs those directions point to.

Warm-up: recall from before

From memory first, then tap to check. Mixing speaking and reading is the point.

The pattern

___ は どこ です か？
___ wa doko desu ka?  ·  "Where is ___?"
literally: [place] + は (topic) + どこ (where) + です (is) + か (question)

You already know three of the four glue words from Lesson 1 and the patterns sheet. Only どこ is new:

PieceRomajiJobSay
はwatopic marker: "as for ___" (your "article")
どこdokowhere (the one new word)
ですdesupolite "is"
かkamakes it a question
One spelling trap. The topic marker は is written with the ha character but pronounced wa. Only this particle does that. Everywhere else, は = ha.
Thai anchor. どこですか ends in か, a question marker, exactly like Thai ไหม / เหรอ at the end of a question. And です carries the polite tone of ครับ/ค่ะ. The shape of a polite question is already in your bones.

So the full sentence, out loud:

トイレはどこですか？
Toire wa doko desu ka?  ·  "Where is the toilet?"

And two more to hear the swap in action: 駅はどこですか？  ·  ホテルはどこですか？

Politeness combo. Open with すみません from Lesson 1: "すみません、トイレはどこですか？" is a complete, polite way to stop a stranger and ask. Get attention, then ask.

Reading the signs you're pointed toward

駅 and 出口 aren't just words to say, they're shapes to recognise on signs. That's your survival-kanji goal starting for real. Full set and the station-exit trick live in Survival kanji.

📜 Backstory · exits are "mouths"

出口 (exit) and 入口 (entrance) both end in 口, which literally means mouth, and by extension an opening. So an exit is an "out-mouth" and an entrance is an "in-mouth" — a building's openings imagined as mouths. Big stations name exits by compass + 口: 北口 = North Exit, 南口 = South Exit.
