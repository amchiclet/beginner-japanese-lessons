カタカナ — the five vowels

This starts your reading track. Katakana is the pointy script Japan uses for loanwords, so it's plastered over menus, signs, and every game screen you already look at. Today you learn the five vowels and the long-vowel dash: the skeleton every katakana word hangs on.

How katakana works (30 seconds)

Katakana is phonetic: one symbol = one sound, and the sound never changes. No guessing, unlike English spelling. There are 46 basic symbols; you already met a few as whole words in Lesson 1 (コーヒー, ビール).

Why this is easy for you. Katakana words are almost all borrowed from English, then bent to fit Japanese sounds — the exact thing Thai does (กาแฟ, เบียร์). You already know the English word and you already own the Japanese sounds as a Thai speaker. Reading katakana is just decoding, not memorising meanings. See Your Thai head start.
📜 Backstory · why three scripts at all?

Japan had no writing until it borrowed kanji from China (~5th century). But Chinese couldn't spell Japanese grammar, so scribes began using some kanji purely for their sound. Around the 9th century that trick was simplified two ways: monks clipped kanji fragments into the angular katakana you're learning now, while cursive versions became the rounded hiragana (next). Three scripts, three jobs: kanji = meaning, hiragana = grammar, katakana = foreign words. Fuller story →

The five vowels

Every katakana syllable is built on one of these five sounds. Learn these and you own one fifth of the whole script on day one. Tap 🔊 to hear each; say it back.

The long-vowel dash: ー

A single dash ー means hold the previous vowel longer. That's it. It's everywhere in katakana, so learning this one mark unlocks a huge number of words.

カー
kā (car)  ·  カ (ka) + ー = "kaa", the vowel held for two beats
Another Thai freebie. Held vs short vowels change meaning in Thai (อา vs อะ), so your ear already hears the difference. English speakers routinely miss it. The dash is just Japanese writing down what you already do.
